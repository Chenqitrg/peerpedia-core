Sign in to PeerPedia.  Your password re-derives your signing key.
After logging in, you can write, edit, publish, and review papers.

HOW TO READ THIS HELP
  The examples below show real commands you can copy and paste.
  Everything you should type is shown — just replace the example values
  with your own.

    <angle brackets>   Replace with your own value.  Do NOT type the brackets.
                       peerpedia article show <id>
                       -> you actually type:  peerpedia article show abc12345

    [square brackets]  This part is optional.  You can skip it entirely.
                       peerpedia article list [--mine]
                       -> you can type:  peerpedia article list
                       -> or:            peerpedia article list --mine

    --double-dashes    A "flag" (option) that changes what the command does.
                       --name means "the name comes next"
                       --json means "show the output as JSON"
                       Some flags take a value (--name "My Name")
                       Others work alone (--json, --rich).

  The prompt character > or $ in examples means "type this in your terminal."
  Don't type the prompt itself.

EXAMPLES
  # Basic login — PeerPedia will prompt you for your password.
  peerpedia account login --name "Marie Curie"

  # Login when multiple people share your name.
  # Use --user-id to pick which one you are.
  peerpedia account login --name "Marie Curie" --user-id abc12345

  # Login on a new device by fetching your account from a peer server.
  # Use this after "account register" on your original machine.
  peerpedia account login --name "Marie Curie" \
      --peer https://peer.example.com --user-id abc12345

  # Login without typing your password interactively.
  # Use this in scripts or when you have your password stored elsewhere.
  PEERPEDIA_PASSWORD='s3kr1t!' peerpedia account login --name "Marie Curie"

  # Login and get machine-readable output (for scripts).
  peerpedia account login --name "Marie Curie" --json
  # -> {"id": "abc12345-...", "name": "Marie Curie"}

WHAT HAPPENS AFTER LOGIN
  • PeerPedia re-derives your signing key from your password + stored salt.
  • Your session is saved so you don't need to log in again on this device.
  • PeerPedia automatically syncs your articles with known peer servers.

WHEN TO USE --peer AND --user-id TOGETHER
  When you log in on a brand-new device, PeerPedia may not know who you
  are yet.  Adding --peer <url> --user-id <uuid> tells it to fetch your
  identity from a server first, then verify your password.  You can get
  your user ID by running "peerpedia account whoami" on your original
  device.

TIPS
  • If you get "wrong password", check your spelling.  Passwords are
    case-sensitive.
  • If you forgot your password, you must re-register — there is no
    password reset.
  • If you fail too many times, your account will lock for a few minutes.
    Wait and try again, or use "account recover" if you remember your
    password.
  • After too many failed attempts, PeerPedia suggests "account recover"
    instead of "account login" — this is intentional, and both work the
    same way once you have your password right.
  • After login, PeerPedia syncs your articles automatically.  If nothing
    appears, run "sync pull --server <url>" manually.

FLAGS
  --name       Your display name.  Required.
               Shown in the "Basic login" example.
  --password   Your password.  If you omit this, PeerPedia prompts you.
               You can also set the PEERPEDIA_PASSWORD environment variable.
               Shown in the "Login without typing your password" example.
  --peer       A peer server URL to fetch your identity from when logging
               in on a new device.  Example: https://peer.example.com.
               Shown in the "Login on a new device" example.
  --user-id    Your user UUID.  Needed for disambiguation when multiple
               users share your name, and required when using --peer.
               Shown in the "Login with disambiguation" example.
  --json       Print the result as JSON instead of human-friendly text.
               Default: off.  Shown in the "Login with --json" example.
  --rich       Print the result as formatted Rich text (the default).

SEE ALSO

  account whoami     See your current session
